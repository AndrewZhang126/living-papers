Background

Palmer penguins Males and females of the same species can have different traits related to their role within an ecosystem. This is known as ecological sexual dimorphism. In this paper [], the authors seeks to explore ecological sexual dimorphism among species of Pygoscelis penguins. They examine if foraging niche are more pronounced in sexually dimorphic species by collecting data from chinstrap, Adelie, and gentoo penguins.

Conclusion

Chinstraps were found to be the most sexually size dimorphic, followed by gentoos. The paper also found that male chinstraps and gentoos were also enriched with certain isotopic nitrogen relative to females, thus revealing a foraging niche.
